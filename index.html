The shift

Video stopped being a timeline you drag. Now there are two ways in, and you pick by the job.

01
Edit it yourself, with AI

You stay in the editor. AI fetches the b-roll, the music and the captions; you arrange them by feel. This is Palmier Pro.

Best for a single clip, when you want to see and place every cut yourself.
02
Direct an agent to build it

You describe the edit in words. The agent writes and renders the whole video. You never touch the code. This is HyperFrames.

Best for a series, when every clip must match and you want scale.
Same craft underneath. One you drive by hand, one you direct in words.
Both run in tools you already have in China: Palmier, plus Claude Code, DeepSeek or Qwen for the agent path.2

Why it works

Every cut answers six questions. A machine can now handle four of them. The two that decide the film, emotion and story, are 74 percent, and they stay yours.

51Emotion
23Story
10Rhythm
7Eye
5Plane
4Space
Yours, 74 percent. Emotion and story: the blink, the moment the idea lands and the story turns. No model finds that for you.
Directable, 26 percent. Rhythm, where the eye already is, screen continuity. The machine can place these, and now even guess the cut point.
Editing is not taste you were born with. The mechanics are learnable and directable; the feeling is the one call that stays yours.
Walter Murch, In the Blink of an Eye, the Rule of Six. The weights a cut must honor: emotion 51, story 23, rhythm 10, eye-trace 7, plane 5, spatial 4. Sources on the next two slides.5

The receipts · foundations

None of this is invented. The method sits on decades of editing research you can go read.

In the Blink of an Eye · Walter Murch · 1995, 2nd ed. 2001 · book
The Rule of Six and the blink. A cut is felt emotional punctuation; emotion is 51 percent of the call, story 23. This is the human theory of the cut.
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_the_Blink_of_an_Eye_(book)
Computational Video Editing for Dialogue-Driven Scenes · Leake, Davis, Truong, Agrawala · SIGGRAPH 2017
A machine selects the right take per line and assembles a full edit in seconds, from named editing rules. This is what the AI in the editor actually does: it picks and arranges.
graphics.stanford.edu/papers/roughcut
Learning to Cut by Watching Movies · Pardo, Caba, León Alcázar, Thabet, Ghanem · ICCV 2021
Trained on 255,000 real cuts, a model learns to rank where a cut is plausible from the audio and picture. It proposes the cut point; it does not decide the film.
arxiv.org/abs/2108.04294
The bottom of Murch's list, and the assembly, is what these systems automate. The top, emotion and story, is what they concede they cannot.6

The receipts · the 2026 frontier

The machines are closing on the mechanics fast, and still miss the cut that carries the feeling.

From Long Videos to Engaging Clips (HIVE) · Wang et al. · EMNLP 2025
Turns a long talk into short clips by splitting the job into three: find the highlight, pick the open and close, prune the rest. The clean blueprint for long to short.
arxiv.org/abs/2507.02790
CutClaw: Agentic Hours-Long Video Editing via Music Synchronization · Zhao et al. · arXiv, March 2026
Three agents: plan to the music, ground each shot on the beat, then review for overlap. Condenses hours of footage into a beat-synced cut. The newest shape of the agent editor.
arxiv.org/abs/2603.29664
VEU-Bench: Towards Comprehensive Understanding of Video Editing · Li et al. · CVPR 2025
The skeptic's receipt. Test 11 top video models on recognizing cuts and transitions and they struggle, some below random. They pattern-match; they do not yet understand the edit.
arxiv.org/abs/2504.17828
Two are peer reviewed, EMNLP and CVPR; CutClaw is a March 2026 preprint, strong but a single study. The frontier moves monthly.7

The recipe

Every short is the same seven moves, so you assemble a clip instead of inventing one each time.

1
Cold-open hook
state the point in the first three seconds, before anyone scrolls past
2
Kinetic captions
words pop in on the beat, the key word gilded gold
3
B-roll on the beat
one cutaway per vivid noun, her voice rolling underneath
4
Hero-word slam
one or two a clip, the big word that lands the line
5
Reaction cut-in
cut to the other person nodding, so it feels like a real talk
6
Lower-third credit
name who is speaking; the host is always credited
7
Outro sting
the same closing clip every time, so the series looks like a set
Learn the seven once. Every short you make is some arrangement of them.
The menu both branches build from, whether you place the moves by hand in Palmier or ask the agent for them in words.8

Same habits

The five habits from workshops one and two still run the show; only the medium changed.

  • ✓
    Context first. Point the agent at your footage, the transcript and a reference clip before you ask for anything.
  • ✓
    Plan before building. Write the beats down first. Re-cutting a plan is cheap; re-rendering a wrong video is slow.
  • ✓
    One job per ask. Captions, then b-roll, then the mix. Small asks land; one giant prompt drifts.
  • ✓
    Review every output. Watch the render. If you cannot hear her clearly, it is not done, however good it looks.
  • ✓
    Commit often. Save a version before "make it weirder," so a bad take costs a minute, not the afternoon.
New medium, same habits. That is why this is workshop three, not a fresh start.
If you sat through workshops one and two, you already know how to do this. Today only swaps the output.11

The exercise

In the next twenty minutes you render your first clip from a template you barely touch.

  1. 1
    Pick a template you like from the gallery. Do not start from a blank page.
  2. 2
    Tell the agent your one line and hand it one photo. Talk to it, never edit the code.
  3. 3
    Ask for two moves: a kinetic caption and one hero word.
  4. 4
    Render. Watch it. Ask for one change. Render again.
  5. 5
    Send the clip to one real person before you leave.
Five seconds is a win. We are training the loop, not making a masterpiece.
